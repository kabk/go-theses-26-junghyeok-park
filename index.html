Introduction

While studying in the Netherlands, I often found myself in situations where I had to read texts written in English, not my native language. In these situations, the text itself felt very limited in its ability to immediately convey the book's content, and I naturally began to focus on other aspects. My eyes are constantly drawn to traces left on the paper beyond the text, handwritten notes, lines drawn to cover mistakes, and pages folded to mark specific sections. These traces played a crucial role in my comprehension.

Building on this experience, while selecting a topic and conducting research, I came across a sentence in the book Early Modern English Marginalia that deeply resonated with me. This sentence later served as a reference when explaining marking structures.

“Erik Schmitt was helping to design the first Kindle in 2007 when he inherited some of his grandfather’s books, books filled with notations, comments, tick marks, and translations, with, as Schmitt puts it, the thought process and interests of someone long gone.”

I have always found it extremely difficult to convey my thoughts in English, both in reading and speaking, as much as I do in Korean. It seems I still struggle with this today. Because of this, I have strived to express my ideas through my work rather than words. However, even in this process, I sometimes struggled to convey my thoughts through the work, especially when it was abstract and difficult to understand directly.

Yet, much like my experience reading English books, I began to think that the traces left in a book could serve as a powerful tool for understanding it, even more so than the text itself. Reflecting on why, I realized it is because these traces allow one to sense the flow of thought of those who read the text, making the process of understanding the writing visually apparent.

Reflecting on this experience and tracing my own thought process, I concluded that showing the process can greatly help understanding, and that traces in books can fulfill this role. However, a problem arose here: since the traces left in books are based on a specific individual's thought process, they are difficult to view as a universal structure.

Therefore, I investigated the traces that could be generally left in books and organized their structure, adopting these traces as the primary elements to serve as visual components that reveal the process of my graduation project. Through this thought process, I arrived at the following questions: If the traces left on a paper book are not merely incidental or accidental, but become visual evidence of the process of reading and thinking, how can I use these traces as primary elements to visually show my process? Furthermore, can this visualized process be regarded not just as a record of the process, but as an outcome in its own right?

Context and Background

To find an answer to this question, I began to examine the various found in books in a more structured way. Particularly in marginalia studies, traces left in books, such as notes, underlining, and markings, are described not as mere decoration or secondary elements, but as material records demonstrating the reader's active reading behavior.

For instance, marginalia encompasses diverse traces like handwritten notes, underlining, arrows, and doodles appearing in margins, between lines, on flyleaves, and even on the inside of book spines. These markings physically reveal the reader's interaction with the text.

This perspective connects to the context of artist books, which understand the book not merely as a medium for conveying information, but as a space where structure and reading practices are jointly formed.

Based on these studies, I began to understand the traces appearing in books not as mere supplementary marks, but as structures where the processes of reading and thinking are materially revealed.

Analysis and Development

Based on these observations, I began to classify the traces more structurally. This structure was organized according to action, material result, specific case, and structural attribute so that it could later serve both as the theoretical chapter of this essay and as the structure of the visual system for the actual work.

The first is “Marking,” which directly intervenes on the surface. This deals with traces that capture the moment when thought is physically imprinted on the surface, forming a line-based system.

The second is “Transforming,” which directly alters layers and surfaces. This structural change system deals with traces capturing the moment a plane is physically reconfigured.

The third is “Accumulating,” which deals with the moment when actions acquire temporality and become structured, using density and rhythm as the foundational system.

Discussion and Reflection

Through this process, traces ceased to be merely supplementary elements to the original text and instead functioned as primary visual components constituting the work.

Conclusion

Through this work, I learned that when using any visual element, there must be a valid reason for its inclusion.

In conclusion, this project begins with the premise that traces left on paper books are not mere residues but can become material evidence of thought processes.
